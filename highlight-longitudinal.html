Longitudinal Observation

16 teams · 61 people · 5 months · mixed-method

To understand how co-exploration actually unfolds in collaborative design, I designed and ran a large-scale longitudinal observational study following 16 design teams — 61 people — across five months of real projects. I managed the entire research process as lead researcher: from study design and participant recruitment to data collection, analysis and publication.

To capture the richness of design activity, I built a weekly design-process diary that let teams document their work the way it actually happened — with images, videos, links, text, charts and emotions. For each activity, participants gave a detailed description, attached reference images, chose the people involved, flagged whether it counted as co-exploration (and why), and expressed how they felt with emojis.

I complemented the diaries with weekly interviews to probe key activities and decisions. By the end, more than 1,500 activities had been documented in detail, and I ran group reflection sessions where teams revisited their co-exploration experiences. Careful study design and project management paid off in positive feedback and 100% participant retention.

The qualitative analysis produced a four-dimensional analytical framework and identified five distinct patterns of co-exploration. The quantitative results showed that thriving teams demonstrated both a higher frequency and a greater diversity of co-exploration than struggling teams.

The work was published as Beyond Divergence: Characterizing Co-exploration Patterns in Collaborative Design Processes in She Ji: The Journal of Design, Economics, and Innovation (2025).

Each pattern maps differently across the framework's dimensions — information distribution, diversity of insights, communication types and people distribution.

Turning a constraint into a collaborative opportunity

During analysis I needed a second coder to ensure rigour and guard against bias — but the project had no budget to hire a research assistant. So I proposed a mini-course on Qualitative Data Analysis within the department: students would learn analysis methods on a real research dataset while contributing to the study as second coders.

I developed the course — learning objectives, methods and hands-on exercises built on the study data. The proposal was approved, an EngD student joined, and the collaboration became a genuine win–win: the student gained hands-on research experience, and the study gained a reliable second coder and a more rigorous analysis. It ultimately resulted in a first-author Q1 journal publication.
