Three ways to fold the cream block

Same four chunks — Research Methods & Analysis, the weekly diary entry, the continuous board, and the group reflection — shown three different ways so you can compare the feel of each. Nothing here touches the live page yet.

Concept 1 · Stacking deck

Scroll and each chunk slides up to stack on the one before it, like dealing cards onto a pile. One chunk fills the view at a time; the earlier cards stay pinned just behind, so you can feel the depth of the stack. Best when you want a guided, one-thing-at-a-time read.

Research Methods and Analysis

I designed and ran a five month observational study with sixteen design teams, sixty one students in total, working on open ended design briefs.

Stage 1, weekly diary-interviews: each week every team documented what they had done, then walked me through it in a short interview. Stage 2, a final group reflection on what co-exploration meant to them.

Analysis ran in reverse. Reflexive thematic analysis of the group-reflection data surfaced shared features of co-exploration. These became an analytical framework, used to code 161 diary activities into five patterns.

2 / 4

The weekly diary entry

Each week, every team filled in a shared diary documenting what they had done, then sat down with me for a short interview where they walked me through it. Participants (A) provided a detailed description of each design activity, (B) attached images as references, (C) selected assignees, (D) identified activities as co-exploration and explained their reasoning, and (E) expressed their emotions using emojis.

3 / 4

A continuous board

Once the diary was complete, I conducted a semi structured group interview, prompting participants to walk through their entries and reflect on the week's activities. The diary board was continuous — each week teams could start a new board while revisiting and linking earlier entries, offering a clear view of the whole design process.

4 / 4

Group reflection

At the end of the project, I held a group reflection session. I transformed the digital descriptions of co-exploration activities into physical cards — diary content on the front, key points and quotes on the back. This hands-on interaction refreshed memories and deepened understanding, laying the foundation for the interview questions.

Research Methods stays on top as context. Best when the diary entry and the board are two sides of the same idea.
