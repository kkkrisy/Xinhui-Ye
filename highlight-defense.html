Defense Day

14 January 2026 · Room 0.710, Atlas Building, TU/e

I had imagined this day so many times that when it actually arrived, it felt slightly unreal. The Rector Magnificus entered carrying a mace, leading eight committee members in full academic gowns. I saw the togas and felt a wave of nerves I had not expected.

The work I was about to defend started with a simple frustration. When design teams go remote, something disappears that is very hard to name. Not the meetings, not the tools, not even the communication exactly. It is the quality of exploring together. Fifteen minutes to present, then a 45-minute defense. Then "Hora Est" from the Rector, and the committee filed out. Lu and Tzu-Hui were right there. Then the applause started.

The reception was at Hubble, and I was so grateful to celebrate with the committee, my family, colleagues, and friends. People call the PhD a lonely journey. I was fortunate to have someone at every turn. That day held more emotions than I knew what to do with: nerves, excitement, relief, joy, and at the very end, a small quiet wonder about what comes next.

The thesis book.

The book pulls together four studies into one argument: that supporting collaborative design is less about any one tool and more about creating the right conditions for co-exploration to happen. I designed the cover and illustrated all of its content myself.

This dissertation investigates co-exploration, an emergent, situated experience that unfolds within design teams during collaboration. Co-exploration is best understood not as a prescribed activity, but as an emergent shared experience that arises from the quality of team interaction. Its principal value lies in transforming individual knowledge into collective intelligence, enabling teams to achieve outcomes beyond the sum of their individual contributions.

Inside the book

Chapter 1 — Introduction

This chapter sets up the core question of how tools can better support remote design collaboration, and traces how the research scope narrowed to the prototyping process, where tacit and experiential knowledge is hardest to transfer remotely.

Chapter 2 — Visions and Real-World Experiences

Two studies open the inquiry. In the first, design students and professionals imagined future remote prototyping tools. The second landed in the middle of COVID-19, when remote collaboration stopped being hypothetical. Designers described losing something they called "building on each other's ideas." That loss became the thread the rest of the thesis follows.

Chapter 3 — Limitations of Current Tools

A systematic review revealed two dominant approaches: tools that sync digital and physical prototypes, and tools that let one person guide another remotely. Neither supports the parallel, bidirectional engagement where everyone explores at once. Two constructive design studies tested potential solutions but stayed limited to early-stage tasks.

Chapter 4 — Understanding and Defining Co-Exploration

The core study. Sixteen design teams observed over five months, generating over 1,500 documented activities. From those, 161 instances of co-exploration were identified and analysed in three sequential passes, resulting in a four-dimensional analytical framework and five co-exploration patterns. Teams that co-explored more often tended to have healthier, more creative collaborative processes.

Chapter 5 — Guidelines for Improving Remote Co-Exploration

Eight professional designers confirmed that the five patterns persist in remote settings but are regularly blocked by reduced social interaction and delayed discussions. Interviews and ideation sessions translated the research into the DTC guideline — structured, practical advice for anyone building tools to support remote design teams.

Chapter 6 — Conclusion and Reflection

The thesis pulls the four research phases together, discusses contributions and limitations, and looks forward. Testing the DTC guideline across different cultural and professional contexts remains open. The chapter ends with the questions this research leaves for whoever picks up the thread next.
